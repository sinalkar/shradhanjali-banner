# Product

## Register

product

## Users

Two equal audiences, often in the same moment:

- **Grieving family members** arriving within hours or days of a death. Often on an Android phone with limited time and emotional bandwidth. They need to produce a banner fast without needing to understand anything about the tool.
- **Local print shop operators and community helpers** who handle banner creation for multiple families. More likely on desktop, more technically fluent, but working under time pressure on behalf of others.

Both groups are primarily Marathi-speaking, located in Maharashtra, India. Devanagari script is the mother tongue of the interface, not a localization afterthought.

## Product Purpose

शराधांजलि बॅनर lets families create, customize, and download a printable funeral/memorial announcement banner without needing a designer or print shop. The product exists because death creates an urgent, specific, painful task — and a tool built with care can remove that friction at the worst moment.

Success looks like: a family opens the site on their phone, fills in the details, and has a shareable or printable banner within 5 minutes, without confusion, without errors, without feeling like they wandered into a startup's marketing page.

## Brand Personality

Devotional, warm, familiar.

Rooted in Maharashtrian Hindu ritual culture — the smell of incense, marigold garlands, the warm amber of oil lamps. The interface should feel like it belongs at a memorial, not at a SaaS demo. It is a known, trustworthy space. It does not need to announce itself.

Voice: unhurried, respectful, clear. Uses Marathi naturally. No marketing language. No cheerful micro-copy. Form labels read like a trusted elder guiding you through the steps.

## Anti-references

- **Generic SaaS/startup purple** — the current `#667eea → #764ba2` gradient reads as a tech product, not a place of mourning. Wrong register entirely.
- **Government / bureaucratic forms** — cold, form-for-form's-sake layout with no warmth or cultural grounding.
- **Western funeral parlor aesthetic** — dark navy, black roses, silver filigree. Not the visual culture of Maharashtrian Hindu ceremonies.
- **Overly festive or colorful** — bright rainbow UI, playful illustration, startup illustration style. Inappropriate for grief.

## Design Principles

1. **Grief deserves dignity, not friction.** Every design choice must reduce cognitive load. The tool should be invisible behind the task. Confusion, missing labels, or unclear states are failures of compassion, not just UX.

2. **Rooted in ritual.** Draw from the visual vocabulary of Maharashtrian Hindu memorial culture: marigold amber, deep devotional mauve, sacred geometry, the quality of candlelight. Not as pastiche, but as genuine home.

3. **Devanagari is first-class.** Marathi script, labels, and copy are the primary language of the interface. Sizing, leading, and font choices must honor Devanagari letterforms, not treat them as Latin text in a different encoding.

4. **Warmth through restraint.** Warmth is not achieved through decoration or busyness. It comes from careful color temperature, generous white space, and type that reads with ease. Less is more reverent.

5. **Trust through completeness.** The download must work. The preview must be accurate. Error states must guide, not abandon. At this moment in a user's life, a broken tool is a broken promise.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

- **Mobile-first**: Android phones are the primary device. Touch targets, type size, and form layout must feel native, not zoomed-out desktop.
- **Large, legible UI text**: Older family members use this. Minimum 16px body text, strong contrast, generous line height.
- **Low-literacy support**: Labels and UI copy must be simple. Support with strong visual hierarchy and clear spatial grouping rather than text-heavy instructions.
- **WCAG AA**: 4.5:1 contrast ratio for all body text. 3:1 for large text and UI components.
